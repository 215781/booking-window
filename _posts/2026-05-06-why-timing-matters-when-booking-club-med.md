Two families book the same Club Med resort. Same week in February, same room category, same number of people. One family pays £1,600 more than the other.

That's not a rounding error. It's a second holiday. And it's not unusual.

This is the founding observation behind When To Book. Club Med operates dynamic pricing — the price you see today is not the price someone else saw last month, and it's almost certainly not the price you'll see next month. The question is whether you can get ahead of those movements rather than react to them.

## How Club Med pricing actually works

Club Med sets prices algorithmically, responding to demand signals: how many enquiries have come in for a particular week, how quickly rooms are filling, how far out the departure date is, and what competitors are doing.

The result is a pricing curve that isn't linear. Prices don't simply fall as the departure date approaches (like budget flights), nor do they simply rise as availability thins out (like budget accommodation). They do both, at different points, for different reasons.

Early in the booking cycle — typically 10 to 14 months out — prices often sit at a base level while the resort is barely on anyone's radar. As the season takes shape and enquiries build, prices move up. They may then stabilise, or dip briefly during quieter commercial periods, before rising again as remaining availability becomes genuinely scarce.

The window between "early optimism pricing" and "scarcity pricing" is where the best timing lives. It's often a few weeks wide. Miss it and you're paying for other people's urgency.

## Why the same week varies so much

Half-term and school holiday weeks are the clearest example. A February half-term week at a French Alps resort can shift by several hundred pounds in either direction within a single month — not because the resort has changed, but because the mix of people booking has changed.

When high-income families from the same school catchment all decide to book in the same fortnight, demand spikes and prices follow. When that surge quiets — because the obvious buyers have already committed — prices can ease before the next wave of interest arrives.

The family that paid £1,600 less didn't get lucky. They booked during the quiet patch between surges.

## The problem with monitoring this yourself

You could, in theory, check Club Med's website every few days and note what you see. In practice, nobody does this systematically for more than a week or two. Life intervenes. You forget. You check again when you're ready to commit, not when the data says to commit.

What you need is a record of what prices looked like on every day leading up to your departure — so you can tell whether today's price is historically high, low, or middling for this resort and week. That's not intuition. It's data.

## The practical takeaway

If you're planning a Club Med ski holiday for next winter, the optimal approach is:

1. **Decide early on resort and approximate week** — not to book, but to start watching
2. **Set a price alert** for that resort so you're notified when prices shift meaningfully
3. **Book when the data says now** — not when life pressure or anxiety about availability says now

The £1,600 difference isn't destiny. It's timing — and timing is something you can plan for.
