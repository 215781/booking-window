Club Med Val d'Isère and La Plagne 2100 are both French Alps ski resorts from the same operator. They share a brochure, the same all-inclusive formula, and — in theory — the same ease of booking. In almost every other respect, they are different propositions aimed at different types of skier. And the price data makes that distinction more concrete than any resort description can.

Our tracker has logged daily prices at both resorts since early May 2026. What we see is not just a gap in the headline figures — it is a difference in how each resort's pricing behaves, which has direct implications for when you should book.

## The price gap is wider than most people expect

Val d'Isère is Club Med's most expensive French Alps resort by a significant margin. For two adults on a seven-night stay, our tracking shows a price range of **£3,150 to £13,608** across the 2026/27 season — a spread driven almost entirely by departure date. Christmas week (departing 20 December 2026) was recorded at **£12,040**. New Year week (departing 27 December 2026) held at **£13,608** across every observation. February half-term week (departing 7 February 2027) sat at **£13,244**.

La Plagne 2100, by contrast, recorded a price range of **£2,874 to £3,322** across the same period — for the entire season, not just shoulder weeks. There are no £12,000 Christmas spikes. The resort's pricing architecture is essentially flat across departure dates: what you pay in mid-January is not substantially different from what you pay at Christmas.

That flatness is a feature of La Plagne's status as a year-round resort rather than a winter-only operation. Without the artificial seasonality that drives up peak-week prices at winter-only resorts, the pricing curve is smoother. For families planning a Christmas or New Year ski holiday, the gap between these two resorts is not a rounding error — it is the difference between a £3,000 week and a £13,000 week.

## How prices have moved — and what that tells you

Flat prices are not the same as static prices. Both resorts have seen movement since our tracker began collecting data in early May 2026.

At **Val d'Isère**, the most instructive movement has been on the January 10 departure date. That week opened in our records at **£7,792** and subsequently moved to **£12,026** — a step up of **£4,234**, or just over 54%, in a matter of weeks. The January 31 departure date moved from **£7,086** to **£7,792** (+£706). The December 6 and December 13 departure dates also moved upward by roughly £500 each. In contrast, the early December and mid-March weeks have held flat.

One notable exception: the March 14 departure date opened in our records at **£10,590** before settling to **£6,862** — a downward movement of £3,728. This is the kind of pricing correction that tends to happen when Club Med adjusts early-season pricing after lower-than-expected demand. It is unusual, and we do not present it as a reliable pattern, but it illustrates that prices can move in both directions.

At **La Plagne 2100**, the picture is different. All twenty tracked departure dates moved simultaneously from **£2,874** to **£3,322** — a uniform uplift of **£448** applied across the season in a single pricing update. This is consistent with how Club Med sometimes adjusts its pricing in batch cycles rather than date-by-date. It means La Plagne's prices moved, but they moved together: no individual departure date offered a timing advantage over another during that window.

## What this means for your booking window

For **Val d'Isère**, the data we have so far points clearly toward booking earlier rather than later, particularly for peak-period weeks. January 10, which moved from £7,792 to £12,026 in our tracking window, is the starkest example — anyone who had been watching that date and booked when the price was at the lower level saved over £4,000. The direction of travel on most tracked weeks has been upward.

It is too early in our tracking period to define precise booking windows with statistical confidence — we are still building the data record that will underpin the Favourable, Watch, and Hold signals on the [When To Book Club Med tracker](/clubmed). But the directional finding is clear: at Val d'Isère, later booking has tended to mean higher prices on most weeks we have tracked.

For **La Plagne 2100**, the picture is less clear. The batch pricing movement suggests that the timing advantage at La Plagne may be less about individual date dynamics and more about catching the right cycle before an operator-wide price update. The booking window logic is different — and arguably simpler. Watch for a period of stable pricing, and treat any hint of movement as a signal to act.

## The resort comparison: what you actually get

Choosing between these two resorts is not purely a price calculation — the experiences they offer are genuinely different.

**Val d'Isère** sits at 1,850 metres and connects to the Espace Killy ski area, which it shares with Tignes. Total piste coverage runs to around 300 kilometres, with a vertical drop that suits experienced skiers. The resort town itself is well-established, with strong infrastructure and a reputation as one of France's elite ski destinations. Club Med's property there reflects that — it is positioned at the premium end of their French Alps portfolio, which the pricing confirms.

**La Plagne 2100** — the 2100 altitude designation is significant — sits high above the main La Plagne village and connects to the Paradiski ski area, which links to Les Arcs across the valley. Paradiski covers around 425 kilometres of piste, making it one of the largest ski areas in the Alps. The resort's year-round operation means it functions slightly differently to Club Med's winter-only villages: less of the closed-for-summer feel, different staffing cycles, and a flatter pricing structure that reflects that continuity.

For families with a wide range of ski abilities, La Plagne's access to Paradiski provides genuine range. For advanced skiers looking for challenging terrain and a prestige address, Val d'Isère and Espace Killy is the natural choice.

## Who should book which resort

The choice often comes down to two things: skiing ambition and price sensitivity — and these tend to push in the same direction.

If your priority is challenging, high-altitude terrain and you are prepared to pay the premium that Val d'Isère commands — particularly for Christmas, New Year, and half-term — then Val d'Isère is a defensible choice, and the data suggests booking sooner rather than later will serve you better than waiting.

If your group includes beginner or intermediate skiers, or if you want extensive ski area coverage at a more accessible price point, La Plagne 2100 and Paradiski is a strong option. The lower absolute prices mean the stakes of timing are also lower — though the evidence of batch price movements suggests that monitoring and acting during a stable period is still the smarter approach than assuming prices will fall.

## One thing both resorts have in common

Whatever your resort preference, the founding observation behind this site applies equally at Val d'Isère and La Plagne: two families who book the same resort for the same week and pay materially different amounts for identical holidays is not a hypothetical. It happens because Club Med's pricing is dynamic, and most people book without watching how prices move.

The gap between an informed booking and an uninformed one tends to be larger at Val d'Isère — where the absolute price levels are higher and individual week movements can run into thousands of pounds — but the principle holds at La Plagne too.
